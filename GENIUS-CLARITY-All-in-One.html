U.S. Digital Asset Regulatory Framework

How the two laws fit together — one governs the dollar coin, the other governs the market around every other token. Not legal advice.

↗GENIUS ActLaw in force (July 2025) · governs payment stablecoins ↗CLARITY ActPending bill (Senate, ~2026) · governs crypto market structure
Both follow the customer: they apply to U.S.-based companies — and to any entity worldwide that serves U.S. customers. The rule follows the customer, not the company's address.
1 · What the framework regulates — and what it doesn't

✓ Regulated

  • GENIUS Payment stablecoins — 1:1, redeemable for $1 (e.g. USDC, USDT).
  • CLARITY Crypto tokens — the assets themselves (e.g. ETH, BNB).
  • CLARITY Trading venues — exchanges, brokers, dealers, custodians (e.g. Coinbase, Binance).
  • CLARITY Token fundraising — selling tokens to the public (e.g. an ICO / token sale).

✗ Not regulated (out of scope)

  • Non-custodial DeFi & self-custody — deliberately exempt / protected (e.g. Uniswap, Aave, MetaMask, Ledger).
  • Algorithmic / decentralized stablecoins — left unaddressed (e.g. DAI, LUSD; formerly UST).
  • Non-USD / no-yield / multi-currency stablecoins — out of scope (e.g. a euro or basket stablecoin like EURC, EURe).
2 · Operational security (OpSec)

Beyond the code — keys, treasury & operational risk

  • Supervised key custody — anyone safekeeping private keys must be a federally/state-supervised qualified custodian (GENIUS + CLARITY).
  • Operational-risk capital charge — regulators can require capital specifically to cover "operational risk" — i.e. key & treasury failures.
  • Mandated IT & operational standards — set per issuer risk (with NIST); weak signer hygiene or M-of-N design would breach them.
  • People are in scope — directors, officers & employees ("institution-affiliated parties") covered for credential/access failures; BSA monitoring + block/freeze.
  • Compromise & insolvency response — freeze/seize/burn on lawful order; reserves get holder priority and are excluded from the bankruptcy estate after a hack.
Sales and Marketing: the law now treats how a company protects its keys, treasury, domains and staff as a regulated duty — not just good practice. If keys are stolen or an insider slips and funds are lost, that's an "operational-risk" failure regulators can penalize, and someone is legally on the hook. Our Four Domains — Multisig Ops, Treasury Ops, DNS & Registrar, and Identity & Accounts — are exactly the controls the law now expects.
Ask: "Are the treasury controls you've documented (limits, signers, time-delays) the ones that actually execute on-chain today?"
3 · Smart-contract audit — the practical prerequisite

Never named in the law — yet functionally mandatory

  • 1 · Public & verifiable code — to be a digital commodity the code must be public, so every flaw is exposed.
  • 2 · Custodian operational risk — the code is the risk; custodians demand an audit to avoid capital charges & liability.
  • 3 · The listing filter — exchanges must prove functions (e.g. freeze/burn) work; an audit is the standard proof.
Sales and Marketing: to be listed on a regulated U.S. exchange (or held by a custodian), a token's code must be public. Public code means any flaw is visible — and the custodian is legally on the hook if it gets exploited. So no regulated exchange or custodian will touch a token whose code hasn't been independently checked. That makes the audit the "gate pass" a project needs to reach U.S. exchanges and institutional money.
Ask: "If a U.S. exchange asked you to prove your code is safe before listing it, what would you show them?"
4 · The actors

GENIUS — stablecoin world

  • Stablecoin issuers — banks/subsidiaries, OCC nonbanks, state-qualified (e.g. Circle).
  • Reserve custodians — hold the reserves, segregated (e.g. BNY Mellon, BlackRock reserve fund).

CLARITY — market world

  • Non-stablecoin token issuers — create & offer any non-stablecoin token (e.g. Uniswap/UNI, Chainlink/LINK, a new L1 foundation).
  • Exchanges, brokers & dealers — run the market (e.g. Binance).
  • Customer's asset custodians — qualified custodians holding customer crypto (e.g. Coinbase Custody, BitGo, Anchorage).
  • Non-custodial DeFi & self-custody — exempt; users hold their own keys (e.g. Uniswap protocol, Aave, MetaMask).
5 · Out of scope

What neither act cleanly covers — and where it goes

  • Algorithmic stablecoins → gap: Treasury study only (SEC if sold as an investment).
  • Real-world assets (RWAs) → SEC if a security (treasuries, funds); CFTC if a commodity (gold).
  • Multi-currency / non-USD stablecoins → SEC if it pays yield; out of scope if it doesn't.
  • Traditional securities & tokenized RWAs → stay under the SEC.
  • Non-custodial DeFi & self-custody → deliberately exempt / protected (CLARITY).
6 · The regulators

GENIUS

  • OCC · Federal Reserve · FDIC · NCUA — the issuer's prudential supervisor (pick one by charter).
  • State regulators — smaller issuers (≤ $10B) that opt in.
  • Treasury / FinCEN & SCRC — AML & sanctions; certifies state/foreign regimes.

CLARITY

  • CFTC — lead; digital-commodity spot markets, exchanges, brokers, dealers.
  • SEC — tokens sold as investments until decentralized; keeps anti-fraud.
7 · Cybersecurity & technical standards

GENIUS — prudential IT oversight

  • Regulators set IT, cybersecurity, BSA & sanctions standards (with NIST).
  • Must be able to freeze / seize / burn coins on a lawful order.

CLARITY — transparency & decentralization

  • Public source-code disclosure + transaction-history access.
  • "Maturity" = decentralized, rules-based consensus, no single controller.
  • Self-custody & developer safe harbor protected.
Shared gap: neither act requires a smart-contract / security (code) audit. GENIUS leaves the technical detail to rulemaking; CLARITY relies on transparency and decentralization instead.

GENIUS Act — at a glance

U.S. law in force (July 2025) · governs payment stablecoins · not legal advice

1 · Geographic coverage

Global reach

  • Applies to U.S.-based companies — and to any entity worldwide that serves U.S. customers. The rule follows the customer, not the company's address.
2 · What it regulates

Payment stablecoins

1 : 1 always redeems for $1
  • Fully backed 1:1 by safe, liquid assets.
  • Holders paid first if the issuer fails.
  • A payment tool, not an investment — no interest to holders.
3 · The actors

Who the law regulates

  • Permitted stablecoin issuersThe licensed legal persons allowed to issue the coin — bank subsidiaries, OCC-supervised nonbanks, state-qualified firms (e.g. Circle / USDC).
  • Foreign issuersNon-U.S. issuers serving U.S. users — allowed only if they meet a "comparable" standard (e.g. Tether).
  • Reserve custodiansBanks or trust firms that hold the reserve assets — must keep them segregated from their own funds.
  • Distributors (exchanges & wallets)Platforms that offer the coin to users — may only list GENIUS-compliant stablecoins.
  • AuditorsRegistered accounting firms examine the monthly reserves.
4 · What it does NOT cover

Out of scope — who covers it instead

  • Algorithmic stablecoins → no clean home: Treasury study only (SEC if sold as an investment).
  • Real-world assets (RWAs) → SEC if a security (treasuries, funds); CFTC if a commodity (gold).
  • Multi-currency / non-USD stablecoins → SEC if it pays yield; out of scope if it doesn't.
  • Other crypto tokens (LINK, AAVE, BTC…) → CLARITY (CFTC, or SEC if a security).
5 · Issuer must

Real money, safely kept

  • Hold real reserves in a "safe vault" — cash & short-term U.S. Treasuries.
  • Be audited — financial only — monthly reserve disclosure checked by accountants.
  • Redeem on demand — clear, timely redemption procedures.
  • Follow AML / KYC rules (Bank Secrecy Act) + freeze/seize on lawful order.
  • By backing coins with Treasuries, it reinforces the U.S. dollar.
Note: the GENIUS audit is a financial / reserve audit (do the dollars exist?). It is NOT a smart-contract or security audit — the law does not require any review of the issuer's code.
6 · Cybersecurity & security standards

Required — but the detail is still being written

  • IT & cyber standardsRegulators (OCC/Fed/FDIC) must set operational, IT, cybersecurity, Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) & sanctions standards, tailored to each issuer's risk.
  • Lawful-order capabilityIssuers must be technically able to seize, freeze or burn coins on a court or agency order.
  • Interoperability standardsRegulators may set compatibility & interoperability standards, developed with the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST).
  • Monitoring innovationTreasury to explore APIs, AI & blockchain monitoring; rules for mixing services & DeFi risk.
  • Smart contracts in scopeThe law defines ledgers as "cryptographically secured" and names smart contracts — but whether a code audit is required is left to rulemaking.
Key gap: GENIUS sets the requirement for security standards but leaves the technical detail — including whether a smart-contract audit is mandatory — to the ongoing multi-agency rulemaking.
7 · The regulators

Who you register with — pick ONE

  • You're a national bank (or its subsidiary)→ register with the OCC.
  • You're a state member bank / bank holding company→ register with the Federal Reserve.
  • You're a state non-member insured bank→ register with the FDIC.
  • You're a credit union→ register with the NCUA.
  • You're a standalone nonbank company (e.g. a crypto startup)→ apply to the OCC (federal nonbank route).
  • You're a small issuer (≤ $10B)→ your State regulator (if the regime is certified); move to federal once you pass $10B.
Always, on top (every issuer): Treasury / FinCEN — AML, KYC & sanctions. SCRC (Stablecoin Certification Review Committee, Treasury-chaired with the Fed & FDIC) doesn't take your filing — it certifies whether a state or foreign regime above qualifies in the first place.

CLARITY Act — at a glance

Pending U.S. bill (Senate, ~2026) · governs crypto market structure · not legal advice

1 · Geographic coverage

Global reach

  • Applies to U.S.-based companies — and to any entity worldwide that serves U.S. customers. The rule follows the customer, not the company's address.
2 · What it regulates

Crypto market structure

What falls under the law

  • Crypto tokens — the assets themselves (e.g. LINK, AAVE, UNI).
  • Trading venues — exchanges, brokers, dealers and custodians.
  • Token fundraising — how projects sell tokens to the public.
3 · The actors

Who the law regulates

  • Token issuersThe natural or legal persons who create and publicly offer a token (any token except a payment stablecoin).
  • Exchanges, brokers & dealersThe venues and firms that run the marketplace and execute trades (e.g. Binance).
  • CustodiansFirms that hold customers' crypto and control their keys — a digital vault.
  • Non-custodial / DeFi & self-custodySoftware and users that never hold anyone else's keys — users control their own assets.
4 · What it does NOT cover

Out of scope / exempt

  • Payment stablecoins → that's GENIUS's job.
  • Traditional securities & tokenized RWAs → stay under the SEC.
  • Exempt: non-custodial DeFi software — developers, front-ends, validators, wallets.
  • Protected: an individual's right to self-custody.
5 · Each actor must

What each actor must do

  • Token issuersDisclose project & risk info, raise under a capped limit, and certify the blockchain is "mature."
  • Exchanges, brokers & dealersRegister (CFTC for commodities / SEC for securities), follow conduct rules, and not trade against their own customers.
  • CustodiansKeep customer assets segregated — and never use them (e.g. staking) without the customer's written consent.
  • Non-custodial / DeFiNothing to register — stays exempt as long as it's genuinely non-custodial.
Note: the DeFi exemption depends on being genuinely non-custodial — and the law mandates no code review to qualify.
6 · Cybersecurity & technical standards

Transparency & decentralization — not prudential IT rules

  • Source-code disclosureA blockchain is defined as publicly available source code; issuers must disclose source code & give transaction-history access to qualify for the exemption.
  • "Maturity" is a technical testThe benchmark is governance & security: a transparent, rules-based consensus with no single person or group in control — this is what flips a token from SEC to CFTC.
  • Self-custody & developer safe harborProtects wallet hardware/software; developers aren't money transmitters just for publishing code or running infrastructure.
  • Stablecoins → GENIUS rulesCLARITY adopts GENIUS's stablecoin definitions, so stablecoin IT & cybersecurity standards (developed with NIST) live in GENIUS, not here.
  • Enforcement capabilityShared with GENIUS: the ability to freeze, seize or burn assets on a lawful order.
Key point: CLARITY mandates no smart-contract audit. But because the code must be public & verifiable to be listed, and custodians must manage operational risk, an audit becomes a practical prerequisite to hold or trade the asset — even though the statute never requires one.
7 · The regulators

Who enforces it

  • CFTCLead — digital-commodity spot markets, plus exchanges, brokers & dealers.
  • SECTokens still sold as investments (until decentralized); keeps anti-fraud authority.
8 · Smart-contract audit — the practical prerequisite

Never named in the law — yet functionally mandatory

  • 1 · Public & verifiable code (CLARITY)To be a digital commodity the source code must be public — so every flaw is exposed, and no exchange can claim ignorance of it.
  • 2 · Custodian operational risk (GENIUS + CLARITY)The code itself is the "operational risk." To avoid capital charges and liability for a hack, custodians demand a third-party audit as proof it's managed.
  • 3 · The listing filter (exchanges)Venues are penalized for listing non-compliant assets and must prove functions like freeze/burn actually work — an audit is the standard tool to show it.
Sales and Marketing: an unaudited token can't clear the path to a regulated U.S. exchange or custodian — and that makes the audit the "gate pass" that moves a project from a private token to a U.S.-listable, institution-ready asset.
Ask: "If a U.S. exchange asked you to prove your code is safe before listing it, what would you show them?"
